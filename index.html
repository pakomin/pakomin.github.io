You'll find my fingerprints in every book. every page from every corner of time bears the mark of my hand having held it, the oil from my fingertips seeping into the ink of each letter. such is my presence in the realm of text. from Hesiod to Rumi - none was spared the scrutiny of my eye, the audience of my ear, the rapaciousness of my appetite for text. So staunch was the vigor of my conviction that somewhere, someone has the right words for this agonizing ache inside me, about as old as memory itself.

And so, I tottered through cybernetic jungles, hacking through thickets of wire, killing bugs, and gazing into every screen till my drool made puddles between my feet, hypnotized and in eager want to feel anything other than this void in my stomach. When tired, I slept in concrete caves emblazoned with marble and glass, dreaming of a life in communal kitchens and a full belly, waking up only reluctantly to the realer and acute presence of hunger, insidiously growing and breeding in me the malaise of disquiet, urging me to keep looking.

With this compromised will, I'd pull myself together and resume foraging for newer pages, prying sentences off from each and placing them on my tongue. Sometimes I had to chew; metaphor and propaganda resisting my bite the hardest. And sometimes they posed no resistence; particularly tales of clinical depression and loss. Each sentence invariably dissolved between my teeth and became mush, suffusing this maw and the back of my head with the essence of its taste before being swallowed whole. I consumed indiscrimiately at first, driven by my capacity to digest whatever came my way. A sign, surely, of a sturdy gut which would not fail me when the words I have been looking for came my way. They would turn in my belly, breaking down into axioms and truths, changing the color of my blood from green to red, dispelling confusion doubt from my lungs, from my speech, and I would finally be whole. I would return to civilization and be a human among other humans - understood and accepted. Until then, I'd keep looking, with an empty stomach gnawing at my insides, churning the choicest literature into the singular statement: "not good enough".

Eventually I discovered a greenhouse, home to a spirit tending rhubarb and thyme. Face as round and pale as the moon, I saw it lumbering between planted bok choi and jasmine. I saw it looking at me from inside, clutching a page dense with scribbles in red. I asked to be let in and was allowed with a gesture of loving grace. I looked towards the pages on its person and it, seeing me looking at them, held them out to me . I went nearer and accepted. And then I took a bite, expecting it to nourish like nothing else. Words from a spirit after all. How could I possibly want anything more. Dismay set in rather quickly when it tasted like sugar; simple, cloying, treacly, and only aggravating an urge that i wanted to be done with. I quietly placed the remaining letters in my pocket, and stared at the being which had made them, looked into its eyes, both wet and reflecting the image of someone obviously in distress. I averted my gaze. The pangs of hunger had returned, necessitating the continuation of the search. Not unlike a feral animal, I set myself about in this greenhouse looking for reprieve, looking for whatever it is that i had been denied all my life. I aggressively pulled at the stems of planted lemongrass and chamomile, uprooting them from the soil and chewing onto them expecting flavor and fulllness. The spirit took my left hand and held it briefly, allowing me to sense the stillness in its palm against the agitation of mine. Neither of us had spoken a word that the other could understand but almost everything had been said. it led me to the center of its home - inexplicably a kitchen. it walked up to the flame and cooking on the pan above it was wheat. the spirit handed me a spoon and with the impudence of child, i helped myself to the food at hand, realizing for the first time, what it means to eat.
